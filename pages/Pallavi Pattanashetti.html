1.William Shakespeare-""

William Shakespeare was a famous English playwright and poet from the late 16th and early 17th centuries. He is often called the greatest writer in the English language. His play “Hamlet” is a tragic story about a prince who seeks revenge for his father’s murder. The play explores deep themes like madness, betrayal, and the meaning of life. Through Hamlet, Shakespeare showed his genius in understanding human emotions and creating timeless characters.

2. John Keats-""

John Keats was a famous English Romantic poet from the early 19th century known for his beautiful and emotional language. His poem “Ode on a Grecian Urn” reflects his love for art, beauty, and nature. In the poem, he describes scenes painted on an ancient urn and imagines the stories behind them. He explores the idea that art can capture beauty forever, even when life and people change. The poem ends with the famous line, “Beauty is truth, truth beauty,” showing Keats’s belief in the lasting power of beauty and art.

3.Jane Austen-"Pride And Prejudice"

Jane Austen was an English novelist of the late 18th and early 19th century. Pride and Prejudice is a novel about love, marriage, and social class in Regency England. The story follows Elizabeth Bennet as she navigates family pressures and misunderstandings. It is known for its wit, humor, and keen observation of society. Austen’s work highlights the importance of personal integrity and understanding in relationships.

4.Percy Bysshe Shelley-"Ozymandias"

Shelley was a major Romantic poet of the early 19th century. Ozymandias is a sonnet about a ruined statue in the desert, symbolizing the fleeting nature of power. The poem explores human pride and the inevitability of time. It uses vivid imagery and irony to make its point. Shelley reminds readers that all worldly achievements eventually fade.

5.Samuel Taylor Coleridge- "The Rime of the Ancient Mariner"

Coleridge was a Romantic poet from the late 18th and early 19th century. His narrative poem tells the story of a sailor who suffers after killing an albatross. The poem explores guilt, redemption, and the power of nature. It is famous for its supernatural elements and rhythmic language. Coleridge’s work highlights moral lessons through vivid storytelling.

6.William Wordsworth-"The Prelude"

Wordsworth was a leading Romantic poet of the late 18th and early 19th century. The Prelude is an autobiographical poem reflecting on his life and his love of nature. It explores memory, imagination, and personal growth. Wordsworth emphasizes the spiritual and emotional power of nature. The poem helped define Romanticism in English literature.

7.George Eliot-"Middlemarch"

George Eliot was the pen name of Mary Ann Evans, a Victorian novelist of the 19th century. Middlemarch explores life in a provincial English town, focusing on marriage, politics, and ambition. The novel is known for its psychological depth and realism. Eliot examines social and moral issues in detail. The work is considered one of the greatest English novels.

8.Charlotte Brontë-"Jane Eyre"

Charlotte Brontë was a 19th-century English novelist. Jane Eyre tells the story of an orphaned girl who grows up facing hardship and injustice. The novel explores love, independence, and moral strength. Brontë combines emotional depth with social critique. Jane Eyre remains a classic example of Gothic romance and personal resilience.

9.Lord Byron-"Don Juan"

Lord Byron was a flamboyant Romantic poet of the early 19th century. Don Juan is a satirical epic poem about a young man’s adventures and love affairs. It combines humor, romance, and social criticism. Byron’s style is witty, bold, and often rebellious. The poem reflects both personal freedom and societal observation.

10..Charles Dickens-"Great Expectations"

Charles Dickens was a Victorian novelist of the 19th century. Great Expectations follows the life of Pip, an orphan who dreams of rising above his social class. The novel deals with ambition, love, and personal growth. Dickens vividly portrays Victorian society and its challenges. The story combines adventure, moral lessons, and memorable characters.
